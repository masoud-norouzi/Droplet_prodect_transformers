\section{Purpose}

This note documents the junction branch-decision diagnostic and the follow-up
geometry-violation analysis.  The goal is to separate two related but distinct
failure modes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Decision error:} the model commits to the wrong outgoing branch
  at the junction.
  \item \textbf{Physical geometry error:} the predicted droplet footprint leaves
  the admissible channel mask, measured by ellipse outside-channel fraction.
\end{itemize}

The analysis uses saved validation rollout predictions from:
\begin{verbatim}
outputs/models/rollout_model_comparison/rollout_predictions.npz
\end{verbatim}

No model inference or retraining is involved.

\section{Junction Event Definition}

True junction events are identified from the true future trajectory only.  A
trajectory is counted as a true junction event when it has at least one valid
assignment to an explicitly allowed incoming branch before committing to an
outgoing branch.  The outgoing branch commitment rule is:

\begin{quote}
same outgoing branch assignment for 3 consecutive evaluable rollout steps.
\end{quote}

The outgoing branches are \texttt{left} and \texttt{right}.  The incoming branch
used in this validation set is \texttt{inlet}.  Arbitrary non-outgoing branches
are not treated as incoming, and a trajectory that begins already assigned to
\texttt{left} or \texttt{right} is not counted as a true junction event.

The corrected event finder identified 4,990 true junction events:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{inlet -> left}: 1,555 events
  \item \texttt{inlet -> right}: 3,435 events
\end{itemize}

\section{Geometry Violations for Wrong Branch Decisions}

A geometry violation is defined as a predicted droplet ellipse outside-channel
fraction greater than the configured geometry tolerance.  The table below asks:
among droplets that committed to the wrong outgoing branch, how many also
violated the channel geometry?

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Geometry violations among wrong-branch decisions.}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
Model & Wrong branch & Violating & Fraction & Median peak outside \\
\midrule
History-20 & 243 & 142 & 58.4\% & 0.030 \\
Markovian & 387 & 244 & 63.0\% & 0.053 \\
Geometry-aware & 435 & 225 & 51.7\% & 0.021 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The geometry-aware model has more wrong-branch decisions in this saved run, but
a smaller fraction of those wrong decisions leave the channel.  Its median peak
outside-channel fraction is also lower than the Markovian baseline.  This
suggests that the geometry-aware model may reduce physically impossible wrong
rollouts even when it does not correct the branch-choice decision itself.

\section{Geometry Violations for Correct Branch Decisions}

The same geometry test was repeated for droplets whose predicted branch decision
was correct.  This asks whether the model chooses the right outgoing channel but
still places the droplet footprint outside the admissible channel.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Geometry violations among correct-branch decisions.}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
Model & Correct branch & Violating & Fraction & Median peak outside \\
\midrule
History-20 & 4,715 & 687 & 14.6\% & 0.000 \\
Markovian & 4,514 & 1,596 & 35.4\% & 0.005 \\
Geometry-aware & 4,498 & 2,801 & 62.3\% & 0.046 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

This is the surprising result.  In this saved prediction set, the geometry-aware
model often predicts the correct outgoing branch but still violates the channel
footprint criterion.  This points to a placement or wall-clearance failure rather
than a branch-selection failure: the model may be choosing the correct channel
while placing droplets too close to, or partially outside, the detected channel
boundary.

\section{Interpretation}

The decision and geometry diagnostics together show that branch choice and
physical admissibility are not the same error mode.  The history-based model has
the best branch-decision accuracy and the lowest rate of geometry violation
among correct decisions.  The Markovian model is worse on both branch decisions
and physical placement.  The geometry-aware model reduces the severity of
wrong-branch geometry violations, but in this saved run it has a high rate of
geometry violations even for correct branch decisions.

The next useful diagnostic is to inspect geometry-aware correct-branch events
with large outside-channel fractions.  These cases should reveal whether the
model is systematically hugging one wall, cutting corners at the junction, or
using bounding-box footprints that are too large for the local channel width.
